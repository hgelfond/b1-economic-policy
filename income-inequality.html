    Wage Inequality

    The chart above shows the evolution of intersectoral wage inequality in the five boroughs in recent years. Mayor Bill de Blasio ran on a platform of ending the "Tale of Two Cities" in New York. Sympathizers and critics alike, however, note that local income inequality is not a direct operating variable of city government. Unlike national and international income inequality, local inequality in a booming city is a reflection of a city planning regime's capacity to make room for rich and poor alike--rather than displacing the poor to a neighboring jurisdiction. New York's rapid economic growth and status as a global "superstar city" make it all the harder to balance political interests in equity and growth.




    Lifelong Consequences of Inequality

    One consequence of inequality is a decline in absolute economic mobility, the likelihood that a child will earn more than their parents. Economists Raj Chetty, David Grusky, Maximilian Hell, Nathaniel Hendren and Jimmy Nrang study how mobility in the United States has changed over time.
